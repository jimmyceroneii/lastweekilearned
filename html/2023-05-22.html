Idea of the Week: Running faster doesn’t work

Back when I ran competitively, a terrible habit held me back. I would blast out of the gates like a hare. Then when I inevitably fell apart towards the end, those smart ass tortoises would reel me in.

For years, I kept trying some variation of this tortoise and hare technique. As I got faster, it seemed to work better. I could fly out of the gates and build up more and more of a lead. Yet at a certain point, my talent ran out on me. There’s just no way anyone can survive a dead sprint for a 5k.

After years of my coach telling me to try a new tact, an injury forced me to do so. I blew out my leg and lost more than a year to it. When I came back, I came back ready to try something new.

The results were immediate. Weeks after returning, I won my first ever race (a junior varsity one, but still). What was the secret? Leaving some extra in the tank.

I became the tortoise instead of the hare. The change scared me. I felt nervous loitering around for the first half of the race, worried I wouldn’t be able to hit the gas when I needed to. Over time though, I came to trust I had it in me.

While sprinting out of the gates felt like giving it my all, the reality was that approach didn’t hold space for the potential I held. Starting slower and running a smart race demonstrated trust in my ability. Flying out of the gates really meant running away from me. It meant running away from responsibility.

It looked great, but it worked badly. As I process a recent breakup, I’m realizing I spent most of the relationship trying to run faster. I worked harder, doubled down on what I thought “worked.”

Yet in doing so, I failed to consider alternative approaches. I took the easy route in many ways, just as I had back in my running days. When we give more effort for the same results, the answer is not more effort. The answer is to change your approach.

What are you doubling down on now? Is it giving double the results?
